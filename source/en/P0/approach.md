# Approach

I have always found it easy to learn new concepts by putting them into practice
in a context that can be explored and controlled with ease. When, years ago, I
began teaching, it felt natural to adopt the same approach, perhaps unknowingly
following a method that only later I discovered had been codified in the
_learning by doing_ methodology {cite:p}`freire1982`. This book tries to follow
the same philosophy, introducing topics &mdash; whenever possible &mdash;
within an applied context from the beginning.

```{margin}
The use of science fiction to introduce scientific concepts is not particularly
unusual: two fairly well-known examples are «The Physics of Star Trek»
{cite:p}`krauss` and «The Physics of Superheroes» {cite:p}`kakalios`[^note].
```

To give the discussion coherence, I decided to place all the examples in the
same narrative domain: the multiverse of superheroes. That choice might seem
ill-suited, given that these characters inhabit a world of pure _fiction_. And
yet, whether a concept can be applied to a context has nothing to do with the
physical feasibility of that context: all that matters is stating clearly,
coherently, and precisely the assumptions that describe a given situation. This
approach allows readers to step metaphorically into that situation, use
mathematics to model it and computer science to simulate it, and then explore
it through the scientific method. Ideally, that should make it possible to
extract information and use it to make decisions. Besides being a great deal of
fun, referring to a nonexistent world has another advantage: it makes it harder
to establish a direct connection between _problem instances_ from the real
world and the methods used to solve them, thereby encouraging learning based on
the _critical_ use of methods and tools.

Despite having written this book, I am no expert on superhero culture. I
therefore apologize in advance to readers who know more than I do for any
inaccuracies or mistakes, hoping they will not hinder the understanding of
the concepts discussed. Although I am on somehow firmer ground when it comes to
data analysis, probability theory, and statistics, I cannot rule out the
possibility that I have made mistakes in general as well, though I trust they
are few.
